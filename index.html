My Custom Rasterizer

This rasterizer became one of the first demo-projects to help me get familiar with the DirectX11 API.

However, the project is not a hardware-based renderer calling onto DirectX, but a software-based renderer that makes the CPU shade every pixel after one another. With a system that mirrors the DX-renderpipeline with

1) an Input Assembler).
2) a Vertex Shader)
3) a Rasterizer)
4) a Pixelshader

After I was somewhat satisfied with the work I did, I also added some camera-functionality, some background-rendering & interactive-UI-rendering.

My Custom Raytracer

This is a small demo-project for rendering basic mathematical 3D-shapes (such as spheres, cubes, triangles), and also delivers on rendering a triangle-mesh, parsed from a .obj file. Making the CPU render every pixel in succesion, made the basic concept of how raytracing works more clear.

It served a good lesson on not alone programming, but also basic-math about how light shades 'pixels' in the real world (PBR materials), and just as important, how the pixels that would not be touched by the lights would have to be shaded.

Finally, after getting a somewhat pleasant result, I tried my hand at some optimization-features on rendering meshes, such as boundingspheres that enabled the 'pre-culling' a healthy amount of rays that were 'obviously' not going to hit the mesh.

PolyBusted!

This was my first experience of working in a semi-professional work-ethic, where I, together with 2 talented artists had to create a game concept, prototype it, and later on, once the development phase came to pass, we had to create our 6-week stretching sprint planning to make every hour of our 'work-day' count.

The game is a 4-player robot-wars inspired arena game in which try to collect scrappy parts that spawn in the arena, and use them a couple of times before they innevitably fall off again. This then makes for opportunity for other players to try and steal it, in order to get the upper hand.

Due to circumstances I ended up being the only programmer of our team pretty early on in development. This meant we had to shrink our production-scale down a bit in order to be able to actually deliver a finished product.
